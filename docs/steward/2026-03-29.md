## 3. Real-World Problem Journal

### 🌿 Sunday: Indigenous Knowledge & Traditional Practices

**Theme: The Fading Art of Kodava Sacred Grove Stewardship**

The Kodava people of Coorg (Kodagu) have maintained *devarkadus* — sacred forest groves — for centuries. These patches of old-growth forest were traditionally protected by community covenant: no tree could be felled, no land cleared, no animal hunted within their bounds. The ecological outcome was remarkable: devarkadus harbor 60–80% higher tree species diversity than adjacent plantation land, serve as seed banks for surrounding agroforestry systems, and maintain perennial water springs that feed downstream paddy fields even through dry months.

**The problem:** Knowledge of devarkadu governance — which trees are ecologically keystone, which species indicate water proximity, which bird-plant relationships signal forest health — lives primarily in the oral traditions of elder Kodava families. As younger generations urbanize, this knowledge is not being transmitted. When a devarakadu's primary custodian family moves away or passes on, the grove frequently falls to encroachment, timber extraction, or simple neglect within a generation.

There is no systematic effort to map the species compositions of these groves, record their traditional management histories, or link them to the broader agroforestry landscapes they support. Forest department surveys treat them as static "reserved areas," missing their dynamic relationship with surrounding coffee estates and cardamom plots.

**Connection to WildArc:**

WildArc's Arbor module already tracks individual trees with species, health observations, and GPS coordinates. The Species database already holds common/scientific names, ecosystem roles, and edible parts. These are the building blocks of a *devarkadu documentation layer.*

A logical extension would be to allow a land or grove to be tagged as `type: sacred_grove` or `type: traditional_knowledge_site`, and to attach:
1. **Oral history records** — transcribed or audio-linked accounts of traditional management practices associated with specific trees or zones
2. **Elder attribution** — credit to the knowledge holder (Kodava family, elder, village) whose expertise informs the record
3. **Seasonal observation protocols** — not just "health score 1-10" but structured phenological records (flowering, fruiting, bird visits, spring flow) that traditional stewards used to gauge grove health

**Concrete feature suggestion:** Add a `knowledge_source` optional field to tree health observations and species records — a text field for attributing traditional knowledge. This is a one-field addition to the existing schema, requiring only a migration column and a UI input. It establishes the principle that WildArc treats traditional ecological knowledge as a first-class data type — not a footnote, but a source on equal footing with scientific literature.

**Longer vision:** WildArc's planned Synapse module (Phase 3, 2027) is designed for mind-mapping ecosystem relationships. A "Traditional Knowledge Graph" — connecting species, seasonal patterns, water sources, elder knowledge holders, and sacred site boundaries — would be a powerful Phase 3 application, and the data to seed it starts with simple attribution fields now.

**References:** Bhagwat & Rutte (2006), "Sacred groves: potential for biodiversity management" — *Frontiers in Ecology and the Environment*; Kushalappa et al. (2012) on devarkadu tree diversity in Kodagu; Nair et al. (2000) on agroforestry systems of the Western Ghats.
