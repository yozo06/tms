This is partially justified — these are drawn directly onto a `<canvas>` element where Tailwind classes can't be applied. However, these values (`#ef4444` = red-500, `#f59e0b` = amber-400, `#22c55e` = green-500) are standard Tailwind colors. They should be extracted into a shared `src/core/constants/actionColors.ts` file with documented mapping to Tailwind equivalents, so they can be kept in sync as the design system evolves.

`MapPicker.tsx:52` uses `#166534` — this IS a forest-800 equivalent, which is good, but should reference the palette constant rather than a raw hex.

### Inline Styles ℹ️

### Small Tap Targets ⚠️

Several icon sizes of `size={10}` and `size={12}` were found in Dashboard.tsx (used alongside `AlertTriangle`, `Clock`, `CheckCircle2` icons in stat labels). These are display-only icons within text spans, not interactive targets, so they don't pose a usability risk. The `HealthLog.tsx:56-57` checkboxes use `w-4 h-4` — these **are** interactive. Per field-first design principles, minimum interactive target size should be `w-5 h-5` or wrapped in a larger touch target. In bright sunlight with muddy fingers, a 16×16px checkbox is genuinely difficult to hit.

## 3. Real-World Problem Journal

### Today's Theme: Seasonal Planning & Crop Calendar Challenges (Monday)

---

There's a question that every food forest owner in the Western Ghats eventually confronts, usually around February when the pre-monsoon heat starts building and the air smells like dry leaves and possibility: *what do I plant, where, and when — and how will I know if I got it right?*

In Coorg, this is not a simple question. The district sits at an elevation where three distinct microclimates can exist within a single property. The southwest monsoon arrives hard, sometimes with 3,000mm of rain between June and September. Then comes a cool dry season, then a brief second flush of rains, then the long dry stretch before summer. Each species in a food forest — and a mature guild might contain 40 or 50 — responds differently to this rhythm.

**The Problem: Multispecies Seasonal Blindness**

Single-crop farmers have a crop calendar. It says: sow in June, harvest in October. Clear enough. But a food forest owner managing 200 trees across 15 species alongside companion herbs, ground covers, and nitrogen fixers has no equivalent tool. The relationships between species are seasonal — a comfrey planted near a mango will behave differently in its first monsoon versus its third year. A moringa that gets cut back in November to protect from cold can actually nurse the soil around a young jackfruit. But if you cut it at the wrong time, you lose the green mulch precisely when the jackfruit needs it most.

In Kodagu, intensification of coffee agroforestry since the 1990s has gradually simplified what were once extraordinarily diverse multispecies systems — some of the highest tree diversity of any coffee landscape in the world — into impoverished monocultures dominated by fast-growing exotics. Part of what drove this simplification was the absence of tools to manage complexity. When you can't track what's happening with 40 species across 4 seasons, simplification feels like rationality.

**Who Feels This:** Yogesh (land owner actively managing the Coorg property — planning across multiple seasons, trying to understand which species interactions are actually working), and Dr. Meera (researcher who wants reproducible seasonal data to study guild performance over time).

**How WildArc Could Help: Seasonal Activity Layer in Arbor/Flora**

This is a gap that sits at the intersection of the Arbor and Flora modules. What's needed is a **Phenological Calendar** — a per-species, per-zone record of seasonal events:

- When does this mango tree flower? (typically Feb–March in Coorg)
- When does the accompanying comfrey hit peak biomass for green mulching? (just before the monsoon flush)
- When should the nitrogen-fixing Gliricidia be pollarded to feed the understory? (pre-monsoon, November)

Right now, WildArc's health observation system captures *what condition* a tree is in on a given day, but not *what season-specific activity* is expected of it. Adding a `phenology_events` table to Arbor — and eventually a visual seasonal timeline per species — would transform WildArc from a health tracker into a genuine seasonal planning tool.

**Data WildArc Needs to Collect:**
- `phenology_events` table: `species_id`, `event_type` (flowering / fruiting / dormancy / pruning_window / mulching_window), `typical_month_start`, `typical_month_end`, `climate_zone`
- Field observations that confirm or contradict the expected window (building real local data over time)
- Zone-level microclimate tags so Yogesh can see that his lower valley zone runs 3 weeks later than the hilltop zone

Over 3–5 seasons of data, WildArc could generate a *farm-specific* seasonal calendar — not generic advice, but what actually happens at this property at this elevation with this soil.

**Inspiration:** The [FAO Crop Calendar](https://cropcalendar.apps.fao.org/) is the gold standard for standardized planting windows, but it operates at regional scale and has no concept of guild interactions or microclimates. Closer to home, [Beforest](https://beforest.co/) — a Coorg-based collective building permaculture food forests — publishes seasonal planting guides, but they remain informal documents rather than structured, data-backed records. WildArc could be what bridges these two worlds: the rigor of the FAO calendar with the ground-truth specificity of Beforest's field knowledge.

*The forest doesn't forget its seasons. Our job is to start remembering them, precisely.*
